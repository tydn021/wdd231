History of Storybook Style!

Back in the early days of Hollywood, the entertainment center in Los Angeles, California, making movies were a huge undertaking. Many fairytale and medieval themed films were shot at this time, and so each set had to be handcrafted from scratch. The builders of these movie sets however would often fall in love with the sets they've created. Another factor that contributed to the rise of early Storybook Style was World War 1. With all of the American soldiers going to Europe a new fondness for Old World architecture began to ignite. But the fondness was especially vibrant for small villages and their quaint stylings.

Many of the set workers would go on to build their own houses in the style of whimsical fairy-tale like homes, and with the aforementioned veterans returning home there was a profoundly large demand for these enchanting European style homes. This boom in interest would fuel the future growth of what has come to be known as "Storybook Style" architecture.

California would obviously be the epicenter for this movement. Arguably the most famous example out of Los Angeles being the Witch's House (pictured above), the house was originally built as an office building for Willat Studios by the artist and set builder Harry Oliver. A film producer named Ward Lascelle liked it so much he decided to buy it and have it moved to Bevery Hills where it is still there to this day.

No brief history of Storybook Style would be complete without a mention of Carmel By The Sea, the first artist commune in America where very talented artists lived and thrived. The artistic creativity would lead to some of the best examples of Storybook Style in the country starting with Hugh Comstock who would go on to build many buildings in Carmel in the Storybook Style.

The style would persist long into the 1930's, but it would see a steep decline afterwards. However sometime in the 1970's and 1980's there would be a revival movement that while rare still see's new houses being built in various places around the world. One of the most recent's was in British Columbia known as the Storybook House by the Sea is also an example of the Tiny House movement.
